1. Team Communication Workshop 25 min

Strong teams communicate with consistency and clarity. You'll practice:

  • Coordinating advocacy messages across team members
  • Smooth transitions between different speakers
  • Maintaining message consistency while showcasing individual strengths
+

Key Terms

Consensus Building: Finding a solution acceptable to all team members.

Active Listening: Fully concentrating on what others are saying, rather than just hearing.

Message Consistency: Maintaining aligned messaging across team members.

Role Clarity: Understanding each team member's contributions and responsibilities.

Discussion Points

  • How does team communication strengthen advocacy efforts?
  • What challenges arise when coordinating advocacy messages across a team?
  • How can you maintain your authentic voice while aligning with team messaging?
  • What communication techniques help teams appear coordinated during presentations?

Activity: Communication Circuit

You'll work in small teams of 3-4 members:

  1. Each team receives a specific advocacy scenario.
  2. Develop a coordinated response that allows each member to contribute.
  3. Assign different roles to each team member, such as:
    • Main presenter (introduces the issue and main points)
    • Data specialist (shares key statistics or evidence)
    • Personal story sharer (provides a human connection)
    • Call-to-action leader (explains what the audience should do)
  4. Practice smooth transitions between speakers.
  5. Present your coordinated message to another team for feedback.

Reflection Questions

  • What was most challenging about coordinating your team's message?
  • How did having different roles strengthen your presentation?
  • Which techniques helped create smooth transitions between speakers?
  • How might you apply these team communication strategies on March 20?

2. Scenario-Based Role Play 30 min

Different audiences require different approaches. You'll practice:

  • Adapting your message for various audience types
  • Identifying audience cues that suggest how to adjust your approach
  • Maintaining your core message while shifting your delivery style
+

Key Terms

Audience Adaptation: Tailoring your approach based on who you're speaking to.

Message Framing: Presenting information in ways that resonate with specific values.

Objection Handling: Responding constructively to concerns or opposition.

Improvisation: Adapting your communication in the moment based on audience response.

Discussion Points

  • What cues help you identify how to adapt your message?
  • How do you maintain your core message while adapting to different audiences?
  • Which audience types do you find most challenging to communicate with?
  • How can you prepare for different audience responses while staying authentic?

Activity: Audience Adaptation Challenge

You'll work in pairs to practice adapting messages for different audiences:

  1. Each pair receives a basic advocacy message.
  2. You'll also receive a series of audience cards describing different types of people (e.g., skeptical administrator, curious student, busy community member, supportive but uninformed parent).
  3. Take turns adapting your message for each audience type.
  4. Your partner provides feedback on your adaptation effectiveness.
  5. Volunteers demonstrate successful adaptations for the whole group.

Reflection Questions

  • Which audience type was most challenging to adapt for? Why?
  • What specific language changes did you make for different audiences?
  • How did your body language and delivery style change across audiences?
  • Which adaptations seemed most effective based on group demonstrations?

3. Call to Action Practice 20 min

Effective advocacy ends with a clear request for action. You'll develop:

  • Concise, powerful 30-second calls to action
  • Techniques for delivering with confidence and conviction
  • Methods for making your request specific and achievable
+

Key Terms

Call to Action: A specific request for your audience to take a defined step.

Conviction: Communicating with belief and passion about your message.

Conciseness: Expressing ideas clearly in a brief format without unnecessary words.

Compelling Delivery: Using voice, pacing, and emphasis to enhance your message impact.

Discussion Points

  • What makes a call to action compelling versus forgettable?
  • How specific should a call to action be?
  • What techniques help you deliver with confidence under time pressure?
  • How do emotional appeals connect to effective calls to action?

Activity: 30-Second Call to Action

You'll create and practice a brief, powerful call to action:

  1. Craft a 30-second call to action related to your advocacy issue.
  2. Include these elements:
    • A clear, specific request
    • A compelling reason why the action matters
    • A sense of urgency or timeline
  3. Practice in small groups with peer feedback.
  4. Focus on clarity, confidence, and persuasive delivery.
  5. Volunteers demonstrate for the whole group.

Reflection Questions

  • What was most challenging about creating a concise call to action?
  • How did your delivery change after receiving feedback?
  • Which calls to action from other participants did you find most compelling? Why?
  • How will you incorporate what you learned into your March 20 presentation?

4. Risk Assessment & Contingency Planning 25 min

Being prepared helps you advocate effectively:

  • Identifying potential challenges for the March 20 event
  • Developing backup plans for various scenarios
  • Building team support systems for unexpected situations
+

Key Terms

Risk Assessment: Identifying potential problems and their likelihood.

Contingency Planning: Developing backup strategies for potential challenges.

Adaptive Response: Adjusting quickly and effectively to unexpected situations.

Team Support Systems: Ways team members can assist each other during challenges.

Discussion Points

  • How does preparing for challenges reduce anxiety?
  • What’s the difference between helpful contingency planning and unhelpful worry?
  • How can team members support each other during unexpected challenges?
  • What are the most common challenges in advocacy presentations?

Activity: "What If?" Scenario Planning

Your team will prepare for potential challenges on March 20:

  1. Each team brainstorms potential challenges that might arise.
  2. You'll also receive "What If?" scenario cards with additional challenges (e.g., tech failure, unexpected resistance, tough questions, attendance fluctuations).
  3. Develop specific contingency plans for each scenario.
  4. Share your most creative and effective solutions with the group.

Reflection Questions

  • Which potential challenge concerns you most about March 20?
  • What contingency plans make you feel most prepared?
  • How can you support teammates who might struggle in certain scenarios?
  • What’s one strategy you’ll definitely use if challenges arise on March 20?

5. Mock Presentation Practice 30 min

Practice makes perfect! You'll rehearse for the March 20 event:

  • Setting up and arranging a presentation table
  • Greeting attendees and starting conversations
  • Handling questions and collecting feedback
+

Key Presentation Elements

Table Setup: Organizing your advocacy materials for maximum visual impact.

Visitor Engagement: Techniques for initiating conversations with attendees.

Question Handling: Methods for addressing inquiries confidently and accurately.

Feedback Collection: Ways to gather input and measure your effectiveness.

Discussion Points

  • What makes a presentation table visually appealing and organized?
  • How do you engage someone who seems hesitant to approach your table?
  • What techniques help you transition from small talk to your advocacy message?
  • How do you balance prepared remarks with spontaneous conversation?

Activity: Event Simulation

You'll practice in a realistic event environment:

  1. Teams take turns setting up a sample presentation station.
  2. Practice these key skills:
    • Table setup and materials arrangement
    • Professional greeting and introduction
    • Delivering key talking points
    • Handling Q&A from "visitors"
    • Collecting feedback or commitments
  3. Other participants play the role of event attendees.
  4. Observers provide constructive feedback.

Reflection Questions

  • What aspects of the presentation felt most natural to you?
  • Which parts were most challenging?
  • Which feedback was most helpful?
  • What will you do differently at the actual March 20 event?
  • What additional materials or preparation do you need?

Your presentation should include:

  • Evidence or data supporting your position
  • A personal connection or story
